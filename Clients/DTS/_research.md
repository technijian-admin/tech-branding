## §02 — How a boutique accounting & finance staffing firm wins

### The dual-sided marketplace

Every staffing firm runs a two-sided business — clients (employers) on one side, candidates
(consultants and job seekers) on the other. Both sides have to be filled, and the firm wins by
matching them faster, more accurately, and with less friction than the alternatives.

**On the employer side**, the funnel is account-based:
1. **DISCOVER** — A CFO or CEO realizes they need a Controller / Senior FA / fractional CFO
2. **QUALIFY** — A first call scoping seniority, comp range, urgency, hybrid posture
3. **MATCH** — DisruptiX returns 2-4 vetted candidate profiles within ~48-72 hours
4. **INTERVIEW** — Employer interviews top candidates; DisruptiX facilitates
5. **AWARD** — Offer extended, accepted, placement made
6. **RETAIN / EXTEND / REPLACE** — Contract role extends, converts to FTE, or replacement cycle begins

**On the candidate side**, the funnel is visibility + relationship:
1. **DISCOVER** — A CPA between roles, returning parent, Big-4 alum looking to go in-house, or
   fractional consultant finds DisruptiX via Google / LinkedIn / referral
2. **APPLY** — Consultant submits resume; KeyMatch portal captures preferences
3. **QUALIFY** — Recruiter screens for fit, runs reference, builds candidate profile
4. **MATCH** — Consultant is matched to client opportunity; warm intro
5. **PLACE** — Consultant accepts engagement
6. **NURTURE** — Between placements, the consultant stays in the DisruptiX network for the next role

**The growth math is multiplicative, not additive.** Every additional client opportunity needs
multiple matchable consultants in the pool, and every additional consultant in the pool needs
a believable pipeline of opportunities to stay engaged. AI helps on both sides at the same time
— matching faster and keeping the pipeline warm.

### Where the cheapest wins live

- **Retained-search direct-hire** — single search, multi-week, $20K-$30K placement fee at
  20-25% of first-year salary on a $90K-$130K accounting/finance hire. High-margin, lumpy.
- **Project / contract consulting** — multi-week engagements, ~$15-$25/hr markup over consultant
  bill rate, typical 6-12-week duration. Volume-driven, predictable cash flow.
- **Renewals and extensions** — the placement that converts from contract to FTE, or the
  contract that extends 90 days, costs almost nothing to win and is the single most profitable
  motion in the business.

## §03 — The compliance & ethics boundary (the DisruptiX analog of VAF/AAVA)

This is the section where the blueprint earns its credibility. AI in hiring is the single most
regulated AI application surface in the US right now, and a recruiting firm that deploys AI
without explicitly naming the boundary loses any sophisticated client the first time the GC asks.

### EEOC AI-in-hiring guidance (2023-2024)

The Equal Employment Opportunity Commission's May-2023 technical assistance document made clear
that an employer (and its staffing partner) is liable for disparate impact created by algorithmic
hiring tools under Title VII, the ADA, and the ADEA. The 2024 update extended this to AI use in
performance management. Practical implication: any AI matching or screening tool used by a
staffing firm must be auditable, source-cited, and explainable — black-box ML scoring of
candidates without a recruiter review-in-the-loop is the textbook liability.

### NYC Local Law 144 — Automated Employment Decision Tools (effective July 2023)

Requires an independent bias audit of any automated tool used to "substantially assist or
replace" a hiring or promotion decision, and candidate notice. The law applies to NYC-based
candidates and jobs but is the de-facto national template — similar laws have been proposed
in Illinois, New Jersey, Maryland, and Washington, and California's SB 7 (2025 session)
extends the concept to employee decisions. DisruptiX matches multi-state candidates; the
audit-ready posture has to hold for NYC even when the firm is SoCal-only.

### California Fair Chance Act (AB 1008) and the FEHA framework

California already prohibits criminal-history inquiries before a conditional offer, requires
individualized assessment, and imposes the broadest worker-protection framework in the country
through the Fair Employment & Housing Act. AI matching that surfaces candidate proxies for
protected characteristics (zip code as race proxy, gap-year as parental-leave proxy) creates
disparate-impact risk. The honest answer is that the recruiter, not the AI, makes any decision
that touches a protected class — and the AI's role is summarization and sourcing, not selection.

### California SB 7 (proposed, 2025 session) — Automated Decision Systems

Would require employer notice and a human-review pathway for any AI tool used in hiring or
employee management. Not yet law but moving — and the right blueprint posture is to be built
the way SB 7 wants the world to look, so the firm's stack does not need a re-architect when
it passes.

### CCPA / CPRA on candidate data

California's privacy regime treats candidate resumes as personal information; the staffing
firm has the disclosure, deletion-on-request, and limited-purpose obligations. AI tools that
index resumes into a vector store create a new processing surface that has to be inventoried
and disclosed.

### The pricing/wages line — what this program will not touch

There is one category of staffing AI that this program explicitly avoids: any algorithmic
wage-setting that shares non-public data across employer competitors or aligns rates among
multiple staffing firms. The same RealPage doctrine that we cite in the AAVA blueprint applies
by analogy — pricing decisions stay with DisruptiX and its clients, and AI may inform comp
benchmarking with public salary survey data but does not feed a multi-firm rate-setting engine.

### The boundary stated plainly

**AI auto-drafts the job spec, indexes the candidate library, summarizes the reference call,
watches the leadership-change signal, and remembers every prior engagement — uniformly, with
sources cited, and inside the review-in-the-loop the regulator expects.** AI never makes the
hiring decision, never adjudicates a protected-class question, and never sets a rate in an
antitrust-sensitive way. A recruiter — and ultimately the client — owns the decision,
explicitly, in writing.

That boundary is the point. It is also the reason DisruptiX wins this conversation against
the giants: a boutique can deploy AI inside the boundary in 2026; a Robert Half cannot
re-platform its global stack inside the boundary in less than 24 months.

## §08 — The silent margin leak — where revenue walks out of a boutique

Three failure modes that quietly compound at any small staffing firm and that the AI engine closes:

1. **Searches that got skipped because there was no recruiter bandwidth.** A boutique team can
   carry a finite number of active retained searches and a finite number of contract-consulting
   placements simultaneously. The result is silent triage: the easy ones get worked, the
   complicated ones get deferred, and a meaningful share of qualified inbound never gets the
   full work. Every skipped search is a placement that went to a competitor with more
   bandwidth, not better candidates. AI-drafted job specs, AI candidate summaries, and AI
   pre-screen video summaries turn the bandwidth ceiling into a non-issue without changing
   the team headcount.

2. **The trigger that nobody caught.** A SoCal mid-market CFO leaves; their replacement gets
   filled by whoever calls the CEO first. A PE portco does a closing announcement; the new
   ops team needs a Controller within 90 days. An IPO filing triggers SOX-readiness staffing.
   A boutique that monitors these signals manually catches a fraction; a boutique that runs
   an AI account-intelligence layer catches them all. The cost of missing one $25K placement
   fee per quarter to a faster competitor compounds into six figures per year.

3. **The consultants who went quiet between placements.** The most expensive vacancy in a
   contract-staffing firm is the consultant who finishes an engagement and then stops
   returning the recruiter's calls — because they took a Robert Half placement that came in
   warmer. The reasons are usually small and avoidable: a check-in that didn't happen, an
   opportunity that wasn't surfaced, a comp shift that arrived as a surprise. None of those
   are relationship failures; all of them are communication and memory failures — which AI-
   enabled consultant-care intelligence and pipeline memory protect against.

These are exactly the failures the AI growth engine closes — and the highest-conviction place
to start, because each converts pipeline DisruptiX already has into placements and renewals
currently being lost.
