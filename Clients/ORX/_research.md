## 00 — Positioning Guardrail (READ FIRST)

**ORX is a known-buyer-universe firm → position the blueprint as Account-Based Marketing (ABM) + account intelligence, NOT broad "digital marketing / lead generation."**

Every orthopedic surgeon, surgery center, PT/pain clinic, and hospital in ORX's SoCal footprint is finite and nameable. Field reps (VP Sales Jamie Kolb + team) already work *named accounts*. That is the definition of an ABM business.

This is the exact lesson from RKE: on 2026-05-21, RK Engineering's president reviewed the 62-page blueprint and **declined the broad-lead-gen framing** — *"we are intentionally not looking for a broad 'shotgun' marketing approach or generalized lead generation."* The capability was right; the positioning was wrong.

**Apply to the ORX blueprint:**
- Do NOT lead with "lead generation," "inbound campaign," or "fill your pipeline."
- DO lead with: **account intelligence** on named target practices/surgery centers/hospitals, **rep enablement** (pre-visit account dossiers), **personalized per-account outreach**, **trigger monitoring** (new surgeons, new surgery centers, expiring competitor contracts), and **AEO/authority content** the specific buyers (practice admins, materials managers, WC case managers) actually read.
- AEO/SEO here is for *credibility and discoverability with named buyers*, not volume traffic.
- Be honest about the boundary: AI supports but does NOT replace the rep relationship (lunch-and-learns, in-services, trust). That honesty is itself a trust signal.

See `feedback_pitch_abm_not_leadgen.md` in tech-branding memory.

## 02 — The Business Model (this is the key to everything)

ORX is **not** a retail DME shop. It is a **B2B2C field-sales + billing operation**. Understanding this is essential — the "buyer" is the provider, the "user" is the patient, and the "payer" is the insurer. Three different parties, one transaction.

### Two divisions

| Division | Customer | How it works |
|----------|----------|--------------|
| **Office Stock & Bill** | Physician practices, ortho/PT/pain clinics, surgery centers (SoCal) | ORX rep monitors inventory at the office, restocks based on the practice's prescription patterns, ships to the office. Provider dispenses directly to the patient at point of care. ORX bills the patient's insurance and owns all follow-up + warranty. |
| **Hospital Stock & Bill Division** | Hospitals (nationwide) | On-site product availability, stocking programs, dedicated field service support. |

### The Stock & Bill value proposition (their words)

> "This will allow you to pull directly from your fully stocked inventory… Helps patients to receive their items much faster than usual… [ORX takes] full responsibility for billing the patient's insurance and also for any follow up."

**Provider benefits ORX sells on:** zero inventory burden, faster patient fulfillment (device handed over in-visit, not days later), reduced admin cost, ORX-owned billing/warranty/follow-up, pricing aligned to Medicare allowable.

### Three revenue-touching functions

1. **Sales / account acquisition** — field reps (Jamie Kolb VP Sales, Stephanie Maginnis Area VP-LA + reps) signing up and retaining provider accounts. *This is the business-development engine.*
2. **Payor Relations Dept** — contract development & administration, account management, payor continuing education. Maintains in-network contracts across dozens of carriers.
3. **In-house billing / RCM** — upfront benefit verification, co-pay explanation, claim submission, denials, follow-up.

### Differentiators they already claim

- In-house billing with **upfront benefit verification** + co-pay explanation
- **In-service training** delivered to facilities/offices
- **Dedicated sales reps** building long-term account relationships
- Product customization & sourcing assistance

| Competitor | Type / Market | Posture vs ORX |
|------------|---------------|----------------|
| **SOS Medical** (Specialized Orthopedic Solutions) | SoCal post-op/pain/rehab + custom O&P manufacturer | Direct regional rival; also manufactures custom O&P (vertical advantage ORX lacks) |
| **Restore Motion** | Orange County, est. 2007, CA/AZ/HI | Direct regional rival in the same SoCal footprint; multi-state |
| **SEE THE TRAINER** | National Stock & Bill for large practices + hospitals | Direct model competitor; targets the same large-practice/hospital accounts |
| **DJO / Enovis** | National manufacturer-owned stock-and-bill (acquired Superior Medical's S&B business) | Scale + manufacturing; can undercut on owned product |
| **Breg** | National manufacturer w/ own VPM/stock-and-bill programs | Partner AND competitor — supplies ORX but also bypasses suppliers via direct programs |
| **Hanger Inc** | National O&P leader (prosthetics/orthotics) | Dominant in prosthetics specifically |
| **MedEx PSI** (Medical Express) | Orthopedic bracing & DME services | Model competitor |
| **AVORS Medical Group** | Lancaster, CA — DME + clinical | Regional, clinic-integrated |

### Gap analysis — white space ORX can own

1. **Digital invisibility across the whole category.** DME stock-and-bill is sold almost entirely by field reps and golf-course relationships. Almost no competitor has a credible inbound/AEO presence for "stock and bill program California" or "[specialty] DME partner." The first player to own AI-search and provider-facing content wins quiet, compounding inbound.

2. **Prior-auth / denial speed as a sales weapon.** Every competitor fights the same enemy: Medicare/commercial denials and prior-auth drag. None markets *speed* of authorization as a differentiator. AI-accelerated prior auth + denial prevention is a concrete, ownable claim providers care about.

3. **Workers' comp case-manager experience.** WC referrals flow through case managers at One Call, Homelink, etc. The supplier that makes the case manager's job effortless (instant eligibility, status, documentation) earns repeat referrals. No competitor appears to have a modern case-manager portal.

4. **Provider-acquisition at scale.** Growth is gated by how many practices a finite field-sales team can visit. AI-driven prospecting + rep enablement multiplies the existing team's reach into Ventura/IE/OC without proportional headcount.

5. **Patient reorder / recurring revenue.** Compression, CPM, supplies, and brace replacements are reorderable, but DME reorder follow-up is notoriously manual. Automated reorder + adherence outreach is recurring margin nobody is systematically capturing.

## 09 — Regulatory Context (include a Regulatory section in the blueprint)

Unlike MWAR (consumer, unregulated), ORX operates in a heavily regulated environment. This *helps* the AI thesis — compliance + documentation burden is exactly what AI removes.

| Domain | Relevance |
|--------|-----------|
| Medicare supplier standards (Palmetto/Noridian) | Supplier number, abbreviated standards posted on-site; audit exposure |
| HIPAA | PHI handling across billing + portals — any AI must be HIPAA-compliant (BAA, no PHI to public LLMs) |
| BOC Gold Seal accreditation | Must maintain benchmarks; documentation rigor |
| Stark Law / Anti-Kickback | Stock-and-bill arrangements must not be a "subterfuge to funnel value to a referral source" — compliant structure matters |
| PDAC / HCPCS coding | Correct product coding drives reimbursement; coding errors = denials |
| CMN / CMS-846 (pneumatic compression), CMS-847 (osteogenesis stimulators) | Documentation forms ORX already references — prime AI automation targets |
| Competitive Bidding / fee schedules | Medicare allowable pricing constrains margin |

**Critical AI guardrail:** Any AI touching patient data must be HIPAA-compliant — Technijian's standard pattern (private/enterprise LLM endpoints, BAAs, no PHI to consumer models). Flag this prominently; it's a buying objection that, handled, becomes a trust signal.
